Lesson 01
Comparing Security Roles & Controls
Who does security? What tools do they use? This lesson sets the foundation — the CIA Triad, NIST functions, security roles, and how controls are categorized and applied.
🛡️
CIA Triad — The 3 Pillars of Security
🔒 Confidentiality
Only authorized people can see the data. Achieved through: encryption, access controls, MFA. Example: Only doctors see patient records.
✅ Integrity
Data is accurate and unmodified. Nobody tampered with it. Achieved through: hashing, digital signatures, file permissions. Example: A prescription can't be altered in transit.
⚡ Availability
Data is accessible when needed. Achieved through: redundancy, backups, load balancing, DDoS protection. Example: The hospital system must be online 24/7.
🖊️ Non-Repudiation
A sender cannot deny having sent a message. Like a signed receipt. Achieved through: digital signatures. "I can prove YOU sent this."
DAD (Opposite of CIA)
Disclosure (breaks Confidentiality), Alteration (breaks Integrity), Denial/Destruction (breaks Availability). Attackers aim for DAD.
Authentication vs Authorization
Authentication = proving who you are (login). Authorization = what you're allowed to do (permissions). These are different! You can be authenticated but not authorized.
💡 Hospital Analogy
Confidentiality = only your doctor sees your chart. Integrity = prescriptions can't be altered. Availability = hospital systems never go down during emergencies.
🧠 Remember
CIA → Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability. Opposite = DAD (Disclosure, Alteration, Denial). Attacks break CIA; defenders protect CIA.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework — 5 Functions
  • 1
    Identify — Know your assets, systems, data, and risks. You can't protect what you don't know about. Includes: asset inventory, risk assessments, governance.
  • 2
    Protect — Deploy safeguards. Firewalls, antivirus, training, access control, encryption, patch management.
  • 3
    Detect — Monitor continuously. IDS/IPS, log analysis, SIEM alerts. Spot threats in real-time or near-real-time.
  • 4
    Respond — React to incidents. Contain the threat, eradicate it, communicate with stakeholders, document everything.
  • 5
    Recover — Restore systems and data. Lessons learned. Update policies. Get back to normal operations.
🧠 Mnemonic
"I Protect Detectives Responding Rapidly" → Identify · Protect · Detect · Respond · Recover

Control Functional Types (What They Do)
🛑 Preventive
Stops the attack before it happens. Firewall rules, access control lists, locked doors, security awareness training, patch management.
👁️ Detective
Detects during or after. Log files, IDS, SIEM, security cameras, motion sensors. Doesn't stop the attack — it NOTICES it.
🔧 Corrective
Reduces damage after attack. Backups and restore, patch management after exploit, antivirus quarantine. Fixes the problem.
⚠️ Deterrent
Discourages psychologically. Warning signs ("Trespassers will be prosecuted"), security cameras in plain view, legal notices. Doesn't physically stop anyone.
🚪 Physical
Guards the physical space. Locks, guards, fences, mantraps, badge readers, lighting. Protects hardware and people.
♻️ Compensating
Alternative when main control can't be used. Example: Can't patch a legacy system → put it behind an extra firewall instead. Same protection, different method.
🧠 Trick — Control Types
P-D-C-D-P-C = "Pretty Detectives Correct Dangerous Perpetrators Continuously" → Preventive, Detective, Corrective, Deterrent, Physical, Compensating
🧪 Quick Check — Section by Section
Q1Which CIA property ensures data has not been tampered with?
✅ Integrity = data hasn't been changed. Think of integrity as "honest data." Hashing proves integrity.
Q2A camera visible outside the entrance discourages attackers. What control type is this?
✅ Deterrent = psychologically discourages. The camera doesn't stop anyone physically — it just makes them think twice.
Q3Your company can't patch an old industrial system. Instead, they isolate it on its own network segment. This is a _____ control.
✅ Compensating = substitute control. When you can't apply the "normal" fix, you use an equivalent alternative.
Q4Which NIST function comes AFTER you detect a threat?
✅ Order = Identify → Protect → Detect → Respond → Recover. After detection comes response (contain & eradicate).
Q5The DAD triad stands for what?
✅ DAD = the attacker's goals, opposite of CIA. Disclosure breaks Confidentiality. Alteration breaks Integrity. Denial breaks Availability.
Lesson 02
Explaining Threat Actors & Intelligence
Who are the bad guys? What do they want? How do they get in? This lesson covers threat actors, attack vectors, and threat intelligence sources.
🎭
Vulnerability · Threat · Risk — Core Definitions
🪟 Vulnerability
A weakness in a system that CAN be exploited. Like an unlocked window. Exists even if no one is trying to use it.
🦹 Threat
The potential for exploitation. A threat actor who COULD climb through that window. A threat needs a vulnerability to become dangerous.
📊 Risk
Risk = Likelihood × Impact. How likely is the burglar to come? How bad if they do? Risk can be: accepted, avoided, transferred (insurance), or mitigated.
💥 Exploit
The actual attack — using the vulnerability to cause harm. Vulnerability + Threat Actor + Exploit = Incident.
🧠 Formula
Risk = Vulnerability × Threat × Impact. No vulnerability = no risk. No threat = no risk. Reduce any factor to reduce overall risk.

Hacker Hat Colors
⚫ Black Hat
Malicious, unauthorized hacking for personal gain or damage. The criminals. No permission, no ethics.
⬜ White Hat
Ethical hackers hired by companies to find vulnerabilities. Full permission. "Penetration testers."
◼ Gray Hat
Hack without permission but don't exploit for profit. May report vulnerabilities. Morally ambiguous. Still illegal.
🔴 Red Team
Authorized attackers who simulate real-world attacks on an organization (more comprehensive than pen testers).
🔵 Blue Team
Defenders — the internal security team that monitors, detects, and responds to the red team and real threats.
🟣 Purple Team
Combination — Red + Blue working together. Red attacks, Blue defends, they share findings to improve security.
Attack Vectors — How They Get In
🖥️ Direct Access
Physical access to the device. Boot from USB, steal the device, access an unlocked workstation.
💾 Removable Media
Infected USB drive left in a parking lot. Curious employee plugs it in → game over.
📧 Email
Malicious attachments or phishing links. Most common attack vector. Requires victim to click.
📡 Remote / Wireless
Cracking Wi-Fi passwords, stealing VPN credentials, exploiting open remote desktop ports.
🏭 Supply Chain
Attack a trusted vendor/supplier to get indirect access to the real target. E.g., SolarWinds hack.
🌐 Web / Social Media
Malicious downloads, drive-by browser exploits, fake social media profiles.
☁️ Cloud
One compromised cloud account can expose the entire organization's data and services.

🧪 Quick Check
Q1Risk is best described as a combination of what two factors?
✅ Risk = Likelihood × Impact. How LIKELY is the bad thing to happen × how BAD would it be.
Q2Which threat actor type typically has the MOST resources and highest skill level?
✅ Nation-state actors (APTs) have the most funding, best tools, and elite skill. They're the most dangerous.
Q3STIX and TAXII are used together. What is TAXII specifically responsible for?
✅ STIX = the language/format (what it says). TAXII = the protocol/delivery system (how it travels). STIX describes, TAXII delivers.
Q4An attacker compromises a software vendor to gain access to the vendor's customers. This is called a _____ attack.
✅ Supply chain attack = target a trusted third party (vendor/supplier) to get indirect access to the real target. SolarWinds is the famous example.
Q5A white hat hacker is different from a black hat hacker because:
✅ The ONLY difference is permission. White hat = hired ethical hacker with written authorization. Same skills, same tools — but legal and sanctioned.

Vulnerability Types
🕳️ Zero-Day
Vulnerability unknown to the vendor. No patch exists. "Day zero" = the day the vendor learns it exists. Most dangerous type.
📋 CVE
Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures. Standardized ID system for known vulnerabilities. Format: CVE-YEAR-NUMBER (e.g., CVE-2021-44228 = Log4Shell). Maintained by Mitre.
📊 CVSS
Common Vulnerability Scoring System. Rates severity 0–10. Critical = 9–10 (patch IMMEDIATELY). High = 7–8.9. Medium = 4–6.9. Low = 0–3.9.
⏱️ Race Condition
A bug where timing matters. Attacker exploits the gap between when a system checks a condition and when it acts on it (TOCTOU — Time-of-Check/Time-of-Use).
⚙️ Misconfiguration
Incorrectly set up systems: default credentials, open ports, overly permissive ACLs. The #1 most common real-world vulnerability.
📦 Unpatched Software
Known vulnerabilities with patches available but not applied. Most ransomware exploits unpatched systems. Patch management is critical.
Penetration Testing — Know the Differences
⬛ Black Box
Zero knowledge. Tester knows nothing about the target. Simulates an outside attacker. Most realistic but time-consuming.
⬜ White Box
Full knowledge. Tester has access to source code, network diagrams, credentials. Most thorough. Fastest. Used in code review.
◼ Gray Box
Partial knowledge. Tester has some info (like a regular user account). Simulates an insider or someone who obtained limited info.
🎯 Active Recon
Directly interacting with the target system. Port scans, ping sweeps, service enumeration. Leaves traces! Can be detected.
🔭 Passive Recon
Gathering info WITHOUT touching the target. Google, LinkedIn, job postings, DNS lookups. No traces. Target never knows.
🔄 Pivot
After compromising one system, use it as a springboard to attack deeper systems behind the firewall. "Island hopping."
💡 Analogy
Pen testing is like hiring a locksmith to try to break into your building and report back. They have your permission. A burglar doesn't. Same skills, totally different legality.
🧪 Quick Check
Q1You want to identify all hosts on a network without completing TCP handshakes. Which Nmap flag do you use?
✅ -sS = SYN/Stealth scan. Sends a SYN, gets SYN-ACK, then immediately sends RST instead of completing the handshake. Stealthy because it doesn't fully connect.
Q2A vulnerability is discovered by a researcher but the software vendor doesn't know about it yet. What is this called?
✅ Zero-day = vendor doesn't know = no patch = extremely dangerous. Once the vendor learns it exists, day zero begins.
Q3A pen tester is given full access to source code and network architecture diagrams before testing. This is a _____ test.
✅ White box = full knowledge. Black = zero knowledge. Gray = partial knowledge.
Q4Searching LinkedIn and company websites to gather info before an attack, WITHOUT touching the target's systems, is called?
✅ Passive recon = no interaction with target. It's like researching someone from public records. Active recon = you're actually probing their systems (leaves logs).
Lesson 04
Social Engineering & Malware
How attackers trick people and infect systems. Social engineering = hacking the human brain. Malware = hacking the machine.
🎣
Social Engineering Principles (Why It Works)
👮 Authority
Impersonating a boss, police officer, or IT admin. People obey authority figures without questioning. "I'm from corporate IT, I need your password."
😤 Intimidation
Threatening negative consequences. "Your account will be suspended unless you act NOW." Creates panic → bad decisions.
👥 Social Proof
"Everyone else is doing this." Fake reviews, "Most employees have already updated." Makes non-compliance seem unusual.
⏰ Scarcity / Urgency
"Limited time only!" "Must respond in 24 hours!" Time pressure bypasses rational thinking. Urgency = reduced scrutiny.
🤝 Familiarity / Liking
Build rapport first. Mention mutual friends, shared interests. "I know your colleague Sarah..." People say yes to people they like.
🎁 Trust
Establish credibility with partial insider knowledge before making a request. Knowing internal project names, org structure.
🧠 Mnemonic
AIS-SFT = "Always Intimidate Students, Scaring Familiar Teachers" → Authority · Intimidation · Social Proof · Scarcity · Familiarity · Trust

Other Social Engineering Attacks
🚪 Tailgating
Sneaking through a secure door without permission, following close behind an authorized person. Employee doesn't realize they let someone in.
🚪 Piggybacking
Attacker follows through a secure door with the employee's knowledge. Employee holds the door, thinking they're being polite. Also wrong!
🗑️ Dumpster Diving
Searching through trash for sensitive documents, old equipment, credentials. Solution: shredding policies, data destruction.
👀 Shoulder Surfing
Watching someone type their PIN or password. Can be done from far away with binoculars or CCTV footage. Solution: privacy screens.
🎭 Pretexting
Creating a completely fake scenario/identity. "I'm the new IT contractor, I need your credentials to set up your account." No real contractor asks for this!
🌊 Watering Hole
Compromise a website the target frequently visits. Wait for them to come to you. Like poisoning a water source animals drink from.
📝 Typosquatting
Register a look-alike domain: "connptia.org" vs "comptia.org". Catches users who mistype URLs.
📞 Impersonation
Pretending to be someone you're not (IT support, delivery person, executive). Can be in-person or over the phone.
Malware Types — Know Every One!
🦠 Virus
Attaches to a host file, spreads when that file runs. Needs user action to spread. Like a cold — needs a carrier.
🐛 Worm
Self-replicating, no host needed. Spreads over networks automatically. Like an airborne disease. WannaCry was a worm.
🐴 Trojan
Disguised as legitimate software. Hides malicious code inside something useful. "Gift" with a spy inside. Does NOT self-replicate.
💰 Ransomware
Encrypts your files, demands cryptocurrency payment for the key. "Pay us or lose everything." Don't pay — no guarantee you get key.
🕳️ Rootkit
Hides deep in OS (kernel level). Grants attackers admin access while evading detection. Very hard to remove — sometimes requires OS reinstall.
🕵️ Spyware
Silently collects data: keystrokes (keylogger), screenshots, browsing history, mic/camera access. Sends data to attacker.
🤖 RAT
Remote Access Trojan. Gives attacker full remote control of victim's machine. Can see screen, access files, use camera/mic.
💻 Botnet
Network of infected "zombie" computers controlled by one attacker (botmaster via C&C server). Used for DDoS, spam, crypto mining.
🔨 Logic Bomb
Malware that activates when a specific condition is met. "If I'm not on the payroll next Friday, delete everything." Often planted by insiders.
📢 Adware
Displays unwanted advertisements. Often bundled with free software. Less malicious but annoying and can track browsing.
💡 Virus vs Worm vs Trojan
Virus = common cold (needs a human carrier/host). Worm = airborne flu (spreads on its own through the air/network). Trojan = gift with a spy inside (you invite it in).
🧪 Quick Check
Q1A finance employee receives an email appearing to be from the CEO asking for an urgent wire transfer. No money was authorized. This is called:
✅ BEC/Whaling — targeting a specific employee with spoofed executive email for financial fraud. Whaling targets the executive; BEC targets employees impersonating the executive.
Q2Malware spreads through a network automatically, exploiting a vulnerability with no user action needed. What type is this?
✅ Worm = self-replicating, no host file, no user action. Virus needs a host file and user to run it. Trojan needs user to install it.
Q3An employee holds the door open for someone they think is a delivery person, letting them into a secure area. This is:
✅ Piggybacking = employee knowingly (but naively) lets the attacker in. Tailgating = attacker sneaks through WITHOUT the employee's awareness.
Q4Malware that sits dormant and executes only when a specific date or condition is triggered is called a:
✅ Logic Bomb = waits for a trigger (date, event, condition) then detonates. Often planted by disgruntled insiders.
Q5Pharming differs from phishing because pharming:
✅ Pharming poisons DNS so even if you type the correct URL, you're redirected to a fake site. No suspicious link to click. Much harder to detect.
Lesson 05
Summarizing Basic Cryptographic Concepts
The math that keeps data secret. Hashing, symmetric & asymmetric encryption, digital signatures, and when to use each.
🔐
Core Vocabulary
Plaintext
Original readable data before encryption. "Hello World." Also called "cleartext" when talking about unencrypted transmission.
Ciphertext
The scrambled, unreadable output AFTER encryption. Meaningless without the key to decrypt it.
Cipher
The algorithm used to encrypt/decrypt. The "recipe" for scrambling. E.g., AES, RSA, Caesar cipher.
Key
The secret value that controls HOW the cipher scrambles data. Longer key = more secure. Key management is critical.
Key Stretching
Making weak keys (like passwords) stronger by running them through algorithms like PBKDF2 or bcrypt thousands of times.
Entropy
Randomness/unpredictability in a key. High entropy = hard to guess. Poor entropy (predictable passwords) = weak encryption.
1. Hashing — One-Way Function
What Hashing Does
Takes ANY input → produces a fixed-length "fingerprint". One-way — you CANNOT reverse a hash to get the original. Same input ALWAYS = same output.
SHA-256
Secure Hash Algorithm. Produces 256-bit hash. The gold standard. Used in TLS, certificates, Bitcoin. Part of SHA-2 family.
SHA-3
Newest SHA standard. Different internal design from SHA-2. More resistant to certain attacks. Being adopted gradually.
MD5
128-bit hash. Broken — do NOT use for security. Vulnerable to collision attacks (two different inputs produce same hash). Still used for file integrity checksums.
Salt
Random value added to password BEFORE hashing. Makes identical passwords produce different hashes. Defeats rainbow table attacks. Stored alongside the hash.
HMAC
Hash-based Message Authentication Code. Combines hash + secret key. Provides BOTH integrity AND authentication. Used in TLS, APIs.
💡 Analogy
Hashing = grinding coffee beans into powder. You can't turn the powder back into beans. Same beans always make same powder (consistency), but many different beans make different powders (collision resistance).
2. Symmetric Encryption — One Shared Key
How It Works
The SAME key encrypts and decrypts. Fast. Good for bulk data. Problem: how do you securely share the key with someone who doesn't have it yet?
AES
Advanced Encryption Standard. The standard symmetric cipher. Block cipher. AES-128, AES-192, AES-256. AES-256 is used by the US government for TOP SECRET data.
3DES
Triple DES. Applies DES three times. Legacy — being phased out. Weaker and slower than AES. Still seen in older payment systems.
Block vs Stream Cipher
Block: encrypts fixed chunks (AES uses 128-bit blocks). Stream: encrypts one bit/byte at a time, great for real-time streaming (RC4 was common — now deprecated).
3. Asymmetric Encryption — Key Pairs
How It Works
Two mathematically linked keys: Public Key (share freely) and Private Key (keep secret). What one encrypts, only the other decrypts.
RSA
Most common asymmetric algorithm. Security based on difficulty of factoring large prime numbers. RSA-2048 minimum today. Slow.
ECC
Elliptic Curve Cryptography. Smaller keys, same security. ECC-256 ≈ RSA-3072. Great for mobile devices. Used in modern TLS.
Diffie-Hellman (DH)
Key EXCHANGE algorithm — allows two parties to establish a shared secret key over an insecure channel WITHOUT ever transmitting the key itself.
💡 Padlock Analogy
Bob distributes unlocked padlocks (public key) to everyone. Alice puts her message in a box, snaps Bob's padlock shut (encrypt with public key), mails it. Only Bob's key (private key) can open it. Nobody intercepting the box can open it.
Digital Signatures — Combining Hashing + Asymmetric
How to Sign
1. Hash the message. 2. Encrypt the hash with your PRIVATE key. 3. Attach this "signature" to the message. Send both.
How to Verify
Receiver decrypts signature using sender's PUBLIC key → gets hash. Receiver also hashes the message independently. If hashes match = valid signature.
What It Proves
Integrity (message not changed) + Authentication (came from claimed sender) + Non-Repudiation (sender can't deny it). Three for one!
Common Use
Software updates (code signing), emails (S/MIME), documents (PDF signing), TLS certificates. Proves the software actually came from the vendor.

🧠 Real World — How HTTPS Uses All Three
1. Asymmetric (RSA/ECC) used to securely exchange a symmetric key during the handshake. 2. Symmetric (AES) used to encrypt actual data (fast). 3. Hashing (SHA-256) used to verify data integrity throughout. Each type does what it's best at!
🧪 Quick Check
Q1You hash "Password123" and get a specific hash value. You add a salt, hash again, and get a different value. What does salting protect against?
✅ Rainbow tables have pre-computed hashes for common passwords. A salt makes each hash unique — the rainbow table no longer matches, making it useless.
Q2To send someone a confidential message using asymmetric encryption, you encrypt with their:
✅ Encrypt with RECIPIENT'S public key → only they can decrypt with their private key. Remember: to sign a document, you use YOUR OWN private key (opposite use case).
Q3ECC-256 provides equivalent security to approximately which RSA key size?
✅ ECC is much more efficient. ECC-256 ≈ RSA-3072. That's why ECC is preferred on mobile and embedded devices — smaller key, same protection, less computation.
Q4A digital signature proves which THREE properties simultaneously?
✅ Digital signatures prove: Integrity (data not changed), Authentication (came from claimed sender), Non-Repudiation (sender can't deny it). Note: does NOT provide confidentiality!
Lesson 06
Implementing Public Key Infrastructure
PKI is the system that issues, manages, and verifies digital certificates — the "ID cards" of the internet. It makes HTTPS and secure email possible.
🏛️
PKI Core Components
📜 Digital Certificate
Electronic document binding a public key to an identity. Contains: subject name, public key, issuer (CA), validity dates, serial number, digital signature. Like a passport for encryption.
🏛️ CA (Certificate Authority)
Trusted third party that issues and vouches for digital certificates. Verifies your identity before signing your certificate. The government that issues passports.
👑 Root CA
Top-level CA. Self-signed certificate. Pre-installed in browsers and OS. Kept OFFLINE for security. The ultimate trust anchor. If Root CA is compromised → catastrophic.
🏢 Intermediate CA
Sits between Root CA and end-entity certs. Does the day-to-day work. If compromised, only its certs are affected — Root CA stays safe. Also called Subordinate CA.
📝 CSR
Certificate Signing Request. You generate this and send to the CA to REQUEST a certificate. Contains: your public key, identity info. The CA signs it and returns a certificate.
📋 X.509
The standard format for digital certificates. Defines what fields a certificate must contain. What you see when you click the padlock in a browser.
🔗 Chain of Trust
Root CA signs Intermediate CA's cert → Intermediate CA signs your cert. Browser verifies each link in the chain all the way up to a trusted Root CA.
🔑 Registration Authority (RA)
Handles identity verification on behalf of the CA. The CA does the signing; the RA does the identity checking. Offloads CA's work.
💡 Passport Analogy
Root CA = Federal Government (ultimate authority, rarely seen directly). Intermediate CA = Regional Passport Offices (do daily work). Digital Cert = Your Passport (proves identity). CSR = Passport Application. Chain of Trust = The authentication hierarchy from your passport back to the government.
Certificate Lifecycle
  • 1
    Enroll — Generate a key pair (public + private). Create a CSR with your public key and identity info. Submit CSR to the CA.
  • 2
    Issue — CA verifies your identity. CA signs the certificate with its private key. Returns the signed certificate to you.
  • 3
    Use — Install certificate. Use it for HTTPS, email signing, device authentication, VPN, etc.
  • 4
    Renew — Before expiration date, renew the certificate (generate new CSR or re-sign existing). Expiration = cert becomes invalid.
  • 5
    Revoke — If private key is compromised, cert stolen, or entity changes — revoke immediately. CA publishes to CRL or OCSP.
Revocation Methods (How to Invalidate a Certificate)
📄 CRL
Certificate Revocation List. A downloadable file from the CA listing all revoked certificates. Downside: can be outdated — only as current as the last download.
🔴 OCSP
Online Certificate Status Protocol. Query the CA in real-time about a specific certificate's status. Returns: Good, Revoked, or Unknown. More current than CRL.
📎 OCSP Stapling
Server fetches OCSP response from CA and "staples" it to the TLS handshake. Client gets revocation status without making a separate OCSP request. Faster & more private.
🧠 CRL vs OCSP
CRL = old method, like downloading a list of banned people (can be outdated). OCSP = modern, real-time check. "Is THIS specific person still allowed?" OCSP Stapling = server pre-fetches and serves the answer for you.

🧪 Quick Check
Q1You visit a website and your browser shows "Certificate Not Trusted." The most likely cause is:
✅ Self-signed certificates aren't in the browser's trusted root store, so they show warnings. Also happens if the certificate chain doesn't trace back to a trusted root CA.
Q2A company needs ONE certificate to cover all subdomains (mail.co.com, vpn.co.com, store.co.com). What type should they get?
✅ Wildcard (*.co.com) covers ALL subdomains. SAN covers specific listed domains. Wildcard = unlimited subdomains; SAN = specific domains you list.
Q3Why is the Root CA typically kept offline?
✅ Root CA offline = air-gapped security. If a Root CA's private key is stolen, EVERY certificate in that chain becomes invalid. Offline = hardest to compromise.
Q4What is OCSP Stapling and why is it better than standard OCSP?
✅ OCSP Stapling = server does the OCSP lookup and includes ("staples") the signed response in the TLS handshake. Client gets revocation info without making a separate request = faster + more private.
Lesson 07
Implementing Authentication Controls
Proving "you are who you say you are." Passwords, biometrics, smart cards, Kerberos, and how attackers try to crack them all.
🗝️
IAM — Identity & Access Management (The 4 A's)
  • ID
    Identification — "Who are you?" Claiming an identity. Providing a username, ID number. No verification yet.
  • AU
    Authentication — "Prove it." Verifying the claimed identity with credentials (password, fingerprint, card).
  • AZ
    Authorization — "What can you do?" Granting or denying access to resources based on permissions/roles.
  • AC
    Accounting / Auditing — "What did you do?" Logging activity for compliance, forensics, anomaly detection.
🧠 Mnemonic
"I Authenticate Authorized Actions" → Identification · Authentication · Authorization · Accounting
Authentication Factors — The 6 Types
🧠 Something You Know
Password, PIN, passphrase, security questions, CAPTCHA patterns. Easiest to steal or guess. Subject to phishing, shoulder surfing, brute force.
💳 Something You Have
Smart card, hardware token (RSA SecurID), TOTP app (Google Authenticator), physical key, PIV card. Can be lost or stolen.
👆 Something You Are
Biometrics: fingerprint, retina scan, iris, facial recognition, voice, palm vein. Can't be changed if compromised. Has FAR and FRR error rates.
📍 Somewhere You Are
Location-based: GPS coordinates, IP address geolocation, physical port, facility access. Used as additional verification factor.
⌨️ Something You Do
Behavioral biometrics: typing rhythm/cadence (keystroke dynamics), gait analysis, how you hold your phone. Enables continuous authentication.
👫 Someone You Know
Web of Trust. Existing trusted users vouch for new ones. Used in PGP/GPG key signing parties. Decentralized trust model.
💡 MFA vs 2FA
MFA = Multiple Factor Authentication = 2+ DIFFERENT factor TYPES. Password + PIN = NOT MFA (both "know"). Password + Fingerprint = MFA (know + are). Password + Phone OTP = MFA (know + have).

Kerberos — Step by Step
  • 1
    AS-REQ — User sends Authentication Service Request to the KDC. Includes username. Password hash used as a key.
  • 2
    AS-REP → TGT — KDC verifies credentials. Issues Ticket Granting Ticket (TGT), encrypted with KDC's secret key. Valid 10 hours.
  • 3
    TGS-REQ — User presents TGT to Ticket Granting Service (TGS) to request access to a specific service/resource.
  • 4
    TGS-REP → Service Ticket — TGS issues a Service Ticket encrypted with the application server's secret key.
  • 5
    Access Granted — User presents Service Ticket to the app server. Server decrypts, verifies, grants access. Password never sent over network!
🧠 Kerberos Key Facts
• Port: 88 (TCP/UDP)
• Requires time sync: clocks within 5 minutes (replay attack prevention)
• TGT default max age: 10 hours
• KDC = single point of failure (use multiple Domain Controllers in AD)
• Provides mutual authentication — both client AND server verified
• Password hash = your "key" to unlock the TGT
Password Attacks — Know All of These!
💪 Brute Force
Try every possible combination. Guaranteed to work given enough time. Defeated by: account lockout, long/complex passwords.
📖 Dictionary Attack
Try words from a wordlist (rockyou.txt, etc.). Much faster than brute force. Defeated by: not using real words, passphrases with numbers/symbols.
🌈 Rainbow Table
Pre-computed database of password→hash pairs. Instant lookup if the hash is in the table. Defeated by: salting (salt makes every hash unique).
🔄 Credential Stuffing
Use stolen username/password pairs from one breach on other sites. Works because people reuse passwords. Defeated by: unique passwords, MFA.
🌊 Password Spraying
Try ONE common password against MANY accounts. Avoids lockout (only 1 try per account). "Password1" tried against 10,000 users. Defeated by: MFA, complex password policies.
🧂 Pass the Hash
Steal the password hash and use it directly to authenticate WITHOUT cracking it. Only works on systems using NTLM authentication. Defeated by: Kerberos, MFA.
🧪 Quick Check
Q1A user logs in with their username (identification) and then provides a fingerprint and a smart card. How many authentication FACTORS are being used?
✅ Identification (username) isn't a factor — it's just claiming an identity. The fingerprint (something you ARE) and smart card (something you HAVE) = 2 factors = 2FA/MFA.
Q2An attacker attempts "Summer2024!" against 50,000 user accounts across your organization. What attack is this?
✅ Password Spraying = one password, many accounts. Avoids lockout thresholds. Best defeated by MFA. Credential stuffing = many unique pairs. Dictionary = many passwords on one account.
Q3In Kerberos, what does the user present to the Ticket Granting Service (TGS) to get a service ticket?
✅ TGT → TGS → Service Ticket → Access. The TGT is your "master ticket" that proves you authenticated. You trade it for specific service tickets. Password is never re-sent!
Q4A biometric system's CER (Crossover Error Rate) is 2%. Another system has a CER of 0.5%. Which is better?
✅ Lower CER = better biometric. CER is the point where FAR (letting wrong people in) equals FRR (rejecting right people). Lower crossover = fewer mistakes overall.
Q5TACACS+ is preferred over RADIUS for device administration because:
✅ TACACS+ encrypts the ENTIRE packet. RADIUS only encrypts the password field. Also, TACACS+ separates auth/authz/accounting (AAA) completely. RADIUS is better for general network access auth (Wi-Fi).
